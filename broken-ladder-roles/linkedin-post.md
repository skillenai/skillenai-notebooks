A new LSE working paper used 243M hire records to argue that remote work — not AI — broke the entry-level tech ladder.

Today's job postings confirm it role by role.

We checked 12 US tech IC roles for the entry-vs-senior remote-allowance gap, then ranked them by AI exposure. If AI were the culprit, AI-heavy roles like Data Scientist, ML Engineer, and AI Engineer should have the steepest broken ladders. They don't.

Every Bonferroni-significant broken ladder in the sample sits in an AI-light or AI-medium role: Software Engineer, Full Stack Engineer, Product Manager, Product Designer. Zero AI-heavy roles show a significant gap.

The widest broken ladders (entry → senior % remote/hybrid):
- Full Stack Engineer: 35% → 72% (+37pp)
- Product Designer: 35% → 65% (+29pp)
- Product Manager: 33% → 57% (+24pp)

The most OPEN ladders for junior data scientists, ML engineers, and AI engineers who want remote work:
- Data Scientist: 43% → 48% (+5pp)
- Research Engineer: 51% → 56% (+5pp)
- ML Engineer: 57% → 69% (+12pp)

The data science and ML engineering roles that look the most "automatable" are not the ones where new grads face the worst entry-level posting market. They're the most open.

The binding constraint isn't AI. It's supervisability — how easy is it to evaluate a junior's work asynchronously? Design taste and full-stack judgment are hard. Research, data science, and backend infra are not.

If you're an employer trying to re-open your entry-level pipeline for data scientists or AI engineers, the lever isn't AI strategy. It's onsite mentorship structure.

Which side of this gradient is your team on?
