In 2019 I went from "associate" to "senior" data scientist in a matter of months. By 2023 I was a principal. By 2025, a senior manager. By 2026, back to a senior IC.

A full round trip through the title ladder. My salary went up the entire way.

I was a walking title bubble. And that bubble is exactly why middle managers are now over-represented in layoffs.

Middle managers were ~20% of layoffs in 2019 and ~32% by 2023, rising every year since 2020 (Live Data Technologies, via Bloomberg). Everyone blames AI. Jack Dorsey blames AI. But the trend started in 2020, before AI could move the labor market at all.

The other popular suspect is remote work. So we tested it against 97,500 US job postings. Three ways. It failed all three:

- Remote postings are no flatter than onsite ones (0.167 vs 0.177 managers per IC).
- Within engineering, becoming a manager costs 0.8 points of remote availability. No cliff.
- Across 251 companies, remote-first firms carry slightly MORE management, not less.

What actually started in 2020 was the unwinding of a title bubble. The ZIRP era of cheap money, a pandemic hiring spree, and the tightest labor market in memory pushed firms to mint managers and hand out senior titles to retain people. In tech, "Lead" and "Principal" titles doubled from 2019 to 2021.

Title inflation never reverses. You can't re-title someone "junior" without losing them. So it corrected the only way it could: through layoffs of the layer that grew the fastest.

The kicker? In our data, executives sit at the bottom of the experienced ranks for remote work — 43%, on par with entry-level and below every senior and management tier. The people issuing RTO mandates never really left the office themselves.

Your title is the weakest signal it has ever been. What's the most inflated title you've seen in the wild?
