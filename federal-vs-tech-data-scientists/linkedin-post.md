A federal data scientist is more likely to have studied epidemiology than a private one — and far more likely to end up at Booz Allen than at Google.

So we stopped looking at job postings and looked at the people. Federal and frontier-tech data science turn out to be two worlds that barely trade members.

Different training. Federal DS come up through domain and social science — epidemiology is their #2 field of study, ahead of statistics. Private DS come up through a concentrated stats-and-engineering funnel. Computer Science is a tie, so federal DS aren't CS-poor — they're domain-heavy.

Different careers. Of 100 federal DS, 64% have held a "researcher" title and 48% an "analyst" title, but only 13% ever an ML/AI title and 11% a software one. Their badges literally read "Statistician (Data Scientist)."

Two closed doors. Of federal DS who joined mid-career, about 46% came from academia and 32% from older-economy industry — but 1 in 76 came from Big Tech. On the way out, 4 of 1,181 went to Google. That is 0.3%.

For the "why can't government hire or keep data talent" debate, the supply side is blunt: there was never a frontier-tech pipeline to backfill from. The skills wall isn't only in the job description. It's in the resume, and the road in and out.
