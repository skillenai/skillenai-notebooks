The market that best explains why new grads can't find jobs isn't tech. It's real estate.

A viral essay this month argued that AI has "torched the market for junior programmers" — that agentic coding tools gutted entry-level software hiring. The collapse it describes is real. Entry-level hiring is down sharply since 2022. But when we pulled the data and asked *why*, the fingerprints didn't point at AI. They pointed at a labor market that **froze** after the 2022 interest-rate shock — in lockstep with the housing market, which nobody accuses of being automated by a chatbot.

Here's the case.

## The collapse is real

![US tech job postings never recovered from the 2022 freeze](https://skillenai-blog-assets-prod.s3.us-east-1.amazonaws.com/uploads/2026/07/05/e955cb79-d6bf-4b65-9c50-5facd727e73f/wyG-YbzJGOc-01-indeed-postings.png)

US software job postings ran near an index of 230 in early 2022, bottomed around 65 in early 2025, and have only crawled back to ~85 — still roughly **63% below peak**. On the hiring side, entry-level software arrivals fell about **56% from 2022 to 2025**, steeper than any senior tier. The more junior the role, the harder it fell.

On this, the essay is right. The disagreement is about the cause.

## But it isn't a tech story

If AI coding tools were the driver, the damage should concentrate in software. It doesn't. Entry-level hiring fell **50–74% across every job function we measured** — and Engineering and IT sit *in the middle of the pack*.

| Function | Change in entry-level hiring, 2022 → 2025 |
|---|---|
| Human Resources | −74% |
| Marketing & Product | −67% |
| **Engineering** | **−65%** |
| Sales & Support | −65% |
| Operations | −64% |
| **Information Technology** | **−63%** |
| Healthcare | −52% |
| Legal | −51% |
| Consulting | −50% |

Human Resources and Marketing got hit *harder* than software. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York's data says the same thing from the graduate side: recent-grad unemployment is elevated across nearly every major, with **anthropology (7.9%) essentially tied with computer engineering (~8%)** at the top. A humanities major and a hardware-engineering major don't get automated by a coding assistant in the same quarter. This is a whole-economy entry freeze.

And within tech, the AI-heavy roles are the *friendliest* to newcomers, not the harshest.

## If AI were killing juniors, AI roles should be worst. They're the best.

![If AI were killing juniors, AI roles should be worst — they are the best](https://skillenai-blog-assets-prod.s3.us-east-1.amazonaws.com/uploads/2026/07/05/e955cb79-d6bf-4b65-9c50-5facd727e73f/U0ygr6QA1xE-05-entry-door-by-role.png)

Across 110,000 US job postings, the share that are entry-level is *highest* in the roles AI is supposed to be eating: Machine Learning Engineer (21%), Data Scientist (16%), AI Engineer (16%) — all matching or beating plain Software Engineer (15%). The genuinely closed doors are lateral infrastructure specializations — Platform Engineer (2.5%), Site Reliability (5.7%), Security (5.4%) — roles that were never entry points to begin with.

We also checked whether entry-level jobs now demand AI skills as a new gate. They don't: AI-skill demand is **flat across seniority** — 25% of entry-level postings mention AI/ML skills, versus 25% of senior postings. Whatever is closing the junior door, it isn't that every entry job suddenly requires an LLM.

## The real culprit: nobody quit

The clearest tell is the **quit rate**. In the tech-heavy Information sector it sits at about **1.2% — below the pre-pandemic norm and below even the pandemic-shock year.** It's a multi-decade low.

That matters more than it sounds. Most entry-level hiring is *backfill*: someone quits, someone gets promoted into the gap, and a newcomer gets hired at the bottom. When incumbents stop quitting, that chain never starts. The vacancies newcomers depend on simply don't open.

You can watch the ladder clog. The first big promotion for a software engineer takes **27% longer** than it did in 2021–22, and the entry-level-to-IC conversion — the first rung — saw **58% fewer promotions**, each taking a third longer. When the people above you stop moving up, there's no room to pull you up. And junior hiring, which is discretionary pipeline-building, is the first line a frozen company cuts.

There's a deeper reason juniors specifically take the hit. **A junior is a complement to senior time, not a substitute for it.** A new hire only becomes productive after a senior spends hours training and reviewing them. Senior engineers' pay never softened in this market — they're the scarce, expensive input. So the fully-loaded cost of a junior (their salary *plus* the senior hours they consume) quietly turned negative. That's the calculation that emptied the bottom rung. (It's also why AI takes the blame — AI competes with juniors for the *same* scarce senior-review attention.)

## Housing is the proof

Why bring in real estate? Because it ran the exact same experiment with **zero AI involved.**

![Two markets, one freeze: homes and jobs both locked up after 2022](https://skillenai-blog-assets-prod.s3.us-east-1.amazonaws.com/uploads/2026/07/05/e955cb79-d6bf-4b65-9c50-5facd727e73f/tQwTlDCEH3U-02-housing-vs-quits.png)

The 2022 rate shock froze the housing market through the same **lock-in** mechanic. About 69% of mortgaged homeowners sit on rates at or below 5% and won't sell into a 7% market. Existing-home sales fell to **4.06 million in 2024 — the lowest since 1995** — and stayed there. First-time buyers, the "entry level" of housing, got locked out — not because they were unwanted, but because the turnover that used to free up starter homes stopped.

That's the labor market's story in a different asset class: freeze the top, and the newcomer at the bottom is the one who never gets in.

And the two freezes are **connected**. A senior engineer sitting on a 3% mortgage won't relocate for a new job — so a whole class of job changes, and the backfill openings they'd create, simply don't happen. Housing lock-in is one of the reasons the quit rate is on the floor.

Housing proves you don't need AI to break the bottom rung. You just need to stop the top from moving.

## What this means for you

**If you're a new grad or job-seeker:** the entry doors that remain are in the roles still creating them — AI/ML, data, and generalist software — not lateral specializations like platform or security that expect prior experience. And watch the quit rate: the entry market thaws when incumbents start moving again, not when AI gets worse at coding.

**If you're a hiring manager:** cutting junior hiring is rational in a frozen market and ruinous over five years — you're not filling the senior pipeline you'll need when it thaws. The firms that keep hiring juniors through the freeze will own the senior talent everyone else is fighting over in 2029.

**If you're a policymaker or economist:** the entry-level crisis is a monetary-policy story wearing an AI costume. It moves with rates and mobility, not model releases.

The analysis is correlational; we show AI's fingerprints are absent from the demand pattern, not that AI has no effect.
