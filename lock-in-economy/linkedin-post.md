New grads can't find work, and everyone blames AI.

But the market that best explains the entry-level job collapse isn't tech. It's real estate.

Here's what the US data actually shows.

Software job postings are down about 63% from their 2022 peak. But the collapse isn't just tech: entry-level hiring fell across HR, marketing, finance, and legal too. Recent-grad unemployment is now elevated across almost every major, with anthropology and computer engineering sitting side by side near the top. A humanities degree and a hardware-engineering degree don't get automated by a coding assistant in the same quarter.

And here's the tell that breaks the AI story. If AI were killing junior roles, the AI-heavy jobs should have the fewest open doors. Instead they have the MOST. Machine Learning Engineer, Data Scientist, and AI Engineer postings are all more likely to be entry-level than a plain Software Engineer role.

So what actually happened? The market froze. BLS data puts the quit rate at a multi-decade low. When senior people stop moving, no backfill openings appear, and the newcomer at the bottom is the one who never gets in.

Which is exactly what happened to housing. The same 2022 rate shock locked homeowners into 3% mortgages they won't give up. US existing-home sales hit their lowest level since 1995, and first-time buyers got frozen out. Same trigger. Same lock-in. Same locked-out newcomer. Zero AI.

The junior job market didn't break because AI learned to code. It broke because the market stopped moving, and a frozen market starves its bottom rung first.

Is the entry-level crisis really about AI, or are we blaming the robot for what interest rates did?
