Jensen Huang told Bloomberg today that AI job-loss fears are "complete nonsense" — software engineers are increasing.

He's half right.

In our US tech corpus — 20,867 individual-contributor (IC) postings, March–May 2026 — the AI labor market is structurally top-heavy in a way generic software engineering is not.

→ AI/ML core (MLE, AI Engineer, Research Scientist): 7.2% entry+junior, 22.9% staff+/principal
→ SW Eng core (SWE, Backend, Frontend, FullStack): 12.9% entry+junior, 12.8% staff+/principal

Same 1.79x ratio, flipped. AI/ML carries 1.79x the staff+ share of generic SWE. Generic SWE carries 1.79x the early-share of AI/ML. Chi-square p < 1e-14.

Fortune today: "the labor market is opening up." Also half right. April→May, both groups gained entry-share (AI/ML +3.5pp, SWE +2.0pp). But the gap didn't close. AI/ML in May (9.9% early) is still below SWE in April (12.9%).

The cluster most exposed to remote work — AI/ML — is also the most senior-heavy. Exactly the mechanism the NY Fed working paper centered: remote work makes juniors harder to train, so firms hire above the entry rung.

The "AI labor market" isn't a level playing field. It's a senior club.

If you're trying to break into AI/ML right now, are you finding more entry-level doors open, or fewer than a year ago?
