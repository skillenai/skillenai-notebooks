A private-sector data scientist has roughly a 1-in-5 chance of losing their job involuntarily this year. A federal data scientist? Basically zero.

That gap is the key to a puzzle. Federal tech roles — data scientists (DS), ML engineers, IT specialists — pay about 30% less than private ones. Yet almost nobody leaves. In a market with few barriers between public and private tech work, a gap that big should close as underpaid workers walk. They don't, so something must be holding them in place.

Using the brand-new OPM federal workforce API, the Skillenai job index, and BLS data, we priced both sides of the public-sector bargain:

Mobility: federal tech workers quit at ~2%/year — one-tenth the private-tech rate, and one-fourteenth of total private.

Security: their involuntary job-loss rate is ~0%/year, versus ~22% in private tech per BLS. A private DS carries a 1-in-5 annual layoff risk. A federal one carries almost none.

The hinge is the pension. Quit rates fall steadily as it vests — 5.5% in your early 20s down to 0.4% near 60 — and retirements erupt exactly at eligibility age. Federal pay is back-loaded: lower salary now, a defined-benefit pension later. That one design choice partly closes the pay gap AND clamps mobility shut.

Even the 2025 cuts fit the pattern: the government shed ~12% of its workforce through voluntary buyouts, not layoffs. Involuntary RIF never topped ~1%.

So is it security, or is it lock-in? The data says both — and the pension is why it can be both at once.
