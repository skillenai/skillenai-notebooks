"The median PM out-earns the median software engineer." That viral Levels.fyi claim is real in the raw numbers. It's also an illusion.

We ran it against advertised pay across the open US job market instead of self-reported comp. Two things were hiding in the pooled median.

First, spam. One employer was carpet-bombing 2,300 identical "Software Engineer" listings at a fixed $140K-$200K band — a full 25% of salaried engineer postings. Strip it out and the pooled engineer median jumps from $170K to $191K, erasing the PM lead instantly.

Second, composition. 44% of everything labeled "Product Manager" is actually a management job (EM, Director, VP). For "Software Engineer" it's 3%. Management pays more, so pooling inflates the PM number. The "PM" bucket isn't richer — it's more senior.

Match them level-for-level and the pattern flips clean. The engineer out-earns the PM at every rung of the IC ladder:

Entry: engineer +$22K. Mid: +$8K. Senior: +$8K. Staff: +$14K. Principal: +$14K.

The senior gap holds up statistically (p=0.0000017, bootstrap CI entirely above zero). And the "generalist" role everyone points to — Product Engineer — pays like an engineer, not a PM. At staff it out-earns straight SWE.

The takeaway: the median PM doesn't out-earn the engineer. The median PM is just more senior.

Two caveats I'll own: this is advertised base, not total comp (equity would widen the engineer's edge, not close it), and Big Tech posts through private systems we can't see, so this is the open market, not FAANG.
