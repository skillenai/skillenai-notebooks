"The median PM out-earns the median software engineer." That viral Levels.fyi claim is real in the raw numbers. It's also a composition trick.

We ran it against advertised pay across the open US job market instead of self-reported comp. Pooled, the two roles basically tie — around $190K each. But that pooled number hides what's actually inside each bucket.

Here's the tell: 44% of everything labeled "Product Manager" is actually a management job — EM, Director, VP. For "Software Engineer" it's 3%. There's barely such a thing as an entry-level PM, so the PM pool sits a full tier higher on the ladder. Management pays a premium, so pooling inflates the PM median. The "PM" bucket isn't richer. It's more senior.

Control for that and the pattern flips clean. Match the same rung to the same rung, and the engineer out-earns the PM at every level of the IC ladder:

Entry: engineer +$22K. Mid: +$8K. Senior: +$8K. Staff: +$14K. Principal: +$14K.

The senior gap holds up statistically (p=0.0000017, bootstrap CI entirely above zero). The advantage is modest but perfectly consistent in sign — engineers don't crush PMs at a level, they just edge them out, every time.

And the "generalist" role everyone points to — Product Engineer — pays like an engineer, not a PM. At staff it out-earns straight SWE. The market prices the "wear both hats" role as engineering.

The takeaway: the median PM doesn't out-earn the engineer. The median PM is just more senior.

Two caveats I'll own: this is advertised base, not total comp (equity would widen the engineer's edge, not close it), and Big Tech posts through private systems we can't see, so this is the open market, not FAANG.
